Growth of Labor Movement
 -National Labor Union
  -Disdained both wage system and strikes but not successful
 -Knights of Labor
  -Critical of capitalism and sought cooperative Socialist measure
 -Significant characteristics
  -Inclusionary
  -Allowed AA, women, skilled and unskilled workers
  -Rejected strikes but later still resorted to strikes
  -Sought social reforms
  -8 hour work day
  -Anti-child labor
  -Crippled by Haymarket Riot
 -American Federation of Labor
  -Adolph Strasser and Samuel Gompers
  -Craft union
  -Rejected unskilled laborers
  -Acceptance of wages/capitalism and selective use of strikes
  -Less centralized, with local guilds
  -Bread and Butter issues
  -Less political/social reforms
  -Wanted to improve wages and conditions

Famous Strikes
 -Great Railroad Strike 1877
  -Riots due to wage cuts of layoffs during Panic of 1873
  -Result was minor wage concessions
 -Haymarket Riot 1886
  -Workers struck against McCornick Company for 8 hour day
  -A bomb exploded during demonstration
  -Led to anti-labor sentiment and decline of Knights
 -Homestead Strike 1892
  -Strike against Carnegie Works
  -Carnegie was not present, Henry Clay Frick was in charge
  -Used Pinkerton Detectives to break the strike
  -6-day war between Pinkertons and workers
 -Pullman Strike 1894
  -Led by Eugene Debs’ American Railway Union
  -Pullman Company tried to fire 40% of workers
  -Attorney General of US deputized 3500 guards to safeguard trains
  -President Cleveland sent injunction against strikers, forcing them to return to work
  -Supreme Court upheld the decision in In Re Debs
  -Legal to force workers to perform their duties if those duties were necessary for interstate trade or the mails

Government Response to Revolution

 -Munn vs. Illinois 1877
  -Led by the Grange, a farmers’ organization
  -Farmers protested RR rebates and price discrimination
  -Supreme Court forbid rebate and state could regulate business practices if it is in the public interest
 -Santa Clara vs. Southern Pacific RR 1886
  -Corporations are people – 14th amendment
 -Interstate Commerce Act 1887/Wabash, St. Louis & Pacific vs. Illinois
  -Response to Railroad Strike and Haymarket Riot
  -Outlawed rebates and pooling
  -Interstate Commerce Commission received the power to force companies to cease from illegal activities, but ICC did not have the power to fix rates, and court rulings undermined its effectiveness
 -Sherman Anti-Trust Act 1890
  -Response to Standard Oil Company
  -Outlawed trusts that restrained trade by price fixing and predatory pricing, but there was no enforcement
 -US vs. E.C. Knight 1895
  -Severely limited governmental regulation
 -In Re Debs 1895
  -Strikes restrain trade
  -Court injunction can be used to break strikes

Roots of Populism

 -Overproduction - Price goes down
 -Market Competition - Price goes down
 -Deflation caused by
  -Limited money supply
  -No coinage of silver
  -Crime of 73 - Dropped bimetallism
 -More debt
 -Decreased the prices paid for agricultural goods
 -Panic of 93
  -Bearing Brothers’ Bank in England touched off a massive financial panic
  -People rushed to banks to redeem their currency, leading national gold reserves to fall to only $41 million
  -Led by J.P. Morgan, a Wall Street syndicate soon arrange an emergency bail out of $62 million, but critics charged that the government was beholden to “special interests”
 -Tariffs increased farmers’ fixed prices
 -Middlemen charged more to transport and store farmers’ goods
 -RR increased the prices that farmers were forced to pay
